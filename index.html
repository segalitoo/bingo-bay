KEY ART Event key art: 10 scene explorations, 1 keeper, framed as a store screenshot ▶
Tool
Nano Banana Pro on Google Flow for generation, manual contrast pass on the cast.
Iterations
10 scene generations (scene_01 to scene_10 in the contact sheet) plus cast group shots. Rejected outcomes: sun too bright and washing out characters, bingo cards too dominant and reading as a casino, cluttered midground stealing focus from the cast, and compositions with no clean band at the top for the event title.
Keeper logic
The selected frame keeps all five faces readable at thumbnail size, holds a clear band across the top for the SUMMER FESTIVAL title, and leads with the world and the cast rather than the mechanic. Thumbnail-size readability was the kill criterion: App Store art is judged at 160 pixels, not 2K.
Critique pass
A review round changed the composition on purpose: the four collectible pets moved to full-body foreground heroes and Mango the host moved up to a supporting corner entrance. The collectibles are what the player chases, so they own the frame; the host fronts the event without upstaging the prizes. The version you see is the post-critique cut.
Manual work
A contrast lift on the cast so faces pop against the sunset sky. The game wordmark is not composited in: this frame ships as an App Store gallery screenshot, so the brand rides on the store icon and title (see the product-page mockup above) and the art carries only the event title.

CHARACTERS Pet cast: 45 generations across 5 characters, one template prompt ▶
Tool
Nano Banana Pro on Google Flow generation, Bria background removal, manual retouch on Mango's edited cutout.
Why Flow
Google Flow lets me fire 4 prompts at once and keeps every result in a scannable gallery, so a whole character explores in one sitting instead of one-render-at-a-time. That batch-and-scan loop kept 55 generations across six families down to a fast afternoon rather than a week of work. The contact sheet below is that gallery, exported.
Iterations
Coral 8, Shelly 8, Twinkle 12, Splash 8, Mango 9. Total 45. Twinkle needed the most rounds: early versions drifted between "starfish with a face" (too flat) and over-detailed renders that broke the collection's soft look. Common rejection reasons across the cast: style drift from the locked render, inconsistent eye language, poses that break silhouette readability at icon size.
Consistency method
Every character prompt is identical except for the bracketed description, so there is one frozen style line and a single variable slot. That keeps the five characters reading as one collection, and it lets a future event extend the cast without re-deriving the look.
Consistency pass
After selection, the five keepers were reviewed side by side against the lock. Splash, Twinkle and Mango got manual AI-assisted retouches (the _edited files) to unify golden-hour lighting, eye highlights and cutout edges. The collection is judged as one set rather than five separate images.

UI · Scene Plate
Mobile game UI background, glossy stylized 3D casual game, summer beach festival at golden-hour sunset, tropical bay with sparkling turquoise water, warm amber sky, paper lanterns and bunting, soft depth of field, no characters, no text, clean composition with clear central space for UI panels, premium casual mobile game art, vertical 9:16 aspect.
MUSIC Event loop: 5 candidates generated, 1 selected, engineered into a game-ready loop ▶
Tool
Suno for generation, then a manual audio-engineering pass (edit, loop, master) outside the model. The model gives raw musical material; a shippable game loop is a separate craft step.
Iterations
5 candidates generated: Bingo at the Beach, Sunny Reception v1, Sunny Reception v2, Sunshine Circuit, and Tropical Welcome. Rejected outcomes and why: Sunny Reception v2 came out the darkest and bassiest, so it read heavier than a summer event wants; Sunshine Circuit was built as a one-shot with a long fade-in that will not loop cleanly; Tropical Welcome had the best name but was slowed to 0.75x, which flattened its energy and left the weakest loop point. Sunny Reception v1 is the polished runner-up, held as the calmer alternate.
Keeper logic
Bingo at the Beach won on measured fit, not vibe alone: across five takes on the same 70 BPM tropical-pop reception brief, it is the brightest and warmest, and the title ties the game and the setting together. Selection was scored on brightness and warmth for a sunny midday reception, plus loop integrity for a long mobile session, rather than a single listen.
Manual work
The raw render was not loop-ready, so I finished it by hand: trimmed the soft fade-in intro that caused a dip on every repeat, built a seamless 81-second loop from the sustained-energy section (35s to 117s) with a 2-second equal-power crossfade wrapping the tail back into the head, and pulled the true peak from a clipping-hot 0.0 dBTP down to a mobile-safe −1 dBTP. The player above is that master.
Close call
My ear preferred Sunny Reception v1, the ukulele felt warmer and more natural, and I nearly led with it. So I re-listened against the real brief: a loop that has to hold up past thirty repetitions, on a phone speaker, without stepping on the win fanfares. On that test Bingo at the Beach stayed pleasant while Sunny Reception started to grate. I would happily hear Sunny Reception once, but I pick for the player who hears it forty times a session. The loop won.
Judgment criteria
Loops without a seam, stays friendly under 30 repetitions, no melody hook so prominent it fights the win fanfares, and sits under the SFX bus without fatiguing.
If more time
The crossfade masks a slightly imperfect beat-phase alignment at the seam. For a truly bar-locked loop I would re-prompt Suno for a shorter, structured stem with a defined bar count rather than editing a through-composed 2.6-minute render, the same "re-prompt over patch" call I would make on any asset.

Part 03AI Workflow Automation

    

    
The brief writes itself.
A human still signs it off.

    
Chosen task: creative briefs. Every asset request becomes a brief, and most eat 45-60 minutes of a lead's day and still arrive half-complete. This automation turns a 2-minute intake into a finished, art-direction-locked brief. The lead approves it instead of writing it.


    

      
Trigger
Intake form
Slack workflow or Jira ticket: asset type, event, deadline, notes. 2 minutes.

      
AI Step
Context pull
Fetches the event's locked art direction, palette tokens and voice guide from the brief library.

      
AI Step
Brief assembly
LLM composes objective, spec, ready-to-run prompts, QA checklist and naming convention.

      
Human Gate
Lead approval
Creative lead reviews in Slack: approve, edit, or reject with a reason that gets logged.

      
Output
Distributed
Brief lands in Jira + Drive, assignee notified, iteration log row pre-created.

    


    

      
Inputs
  • Asset type + event name
  • Deadline and requester
  • Free-text intent notes
  • Brief library: art direction locks, voice guide, past briefs

      
Outputs
  • Structured brief (objective, spec, references)
  • Ready-to-paste generation prompts
  • QA checklist + file naming
  • Pre-created iteration log entry

      
Tools
  • n8n (self-hosted) as the spine
  • Claude API for assembly
  • Slack intake + approval buttons
  • Jira + Google Drive delivery

      
Trigger
New intake submission, or a Jira ticket labeled needs-brief. Nothing runs on a schedule; the queue is event-driven.

      
Human approval
Two gates: the lead approves the brief before distribution, and the art director owns changes to any locked token the brief cites. AI never edits the locks.

      
Time saved · estimate
~7.5 h / week
Assumes 10 briefs a week at 45-60 min each by hand, versus 2 min intake plus 3 min review. Roughly a full workday back for actual creative direction.

Part 04AI Discovery

    

    
Three tools I would actually deploy.

    
Not the obvious picks. Each one is chosen against a specific studio pain: legal exposure on generated art, static UI in a live game, and the concept-to-3D gap of a 3D title.

    

      

        
Bria

        
IP-safe visual generation

        
Tested for this submission
I re-posed the approved Mango in Bria, same character, brand new pose, through my own tool built on the Bria API. IP-safe by default, so nothing about it worries legal.

        

          
          →
          
        

        
One approved pet becomes an idle, a win, a jump, a popup, without redrawing and without an IP question. That kind of reuse is what a live game depends on.

        
What it does
Image generation, editing and re-posing APIs (including the background removal used throughout Part 2) trained exclusively on licensed data, with legal indemnification and attribution built in.

        
Why this one
For a publicly traded studio, the deciding factor is less about which model looks best and more about which output legal can approve. Indemnified generation removes the biggest silent blocker to shipping AI art in production, and the API drops straight into tools the studio already builds.

        
Who uses it
All production artists via API in the asset pipeline; legal sets policy once instead of reviewing per-asset.

        
Limitations
Its raw aesthetic ceiling trails the frontier consumer models, so it works best as the production and editing layer, with exploration done elsewhere.

        
IMPACT
Category change
Unblocks AI art for commercial release, not just concepting.

      

      

        
Rive

        
Runtime interactive animation

        
Live in this card
A pure-CSS stand-in for the kind of juicy button Rive ships as a tiny .riv. It plays its hover on a loop, and still reacts when you actually hover or press it.

        

          

          
        

        
In Rive this is a small state machine (idle, hover, pressed) driven by the runtime: a fraction of a video's size, and editable by a designer without an engineer.

        
What it does
Editor plus lightweight runtime for interactive vector animation with state machines. Event screens, buttons, mascot reactions and progress bars ship as tiny .riv files that run inside the game, not as baked video.

        
Why this one
Casual mobile games depend on juicy UI, and today most of it is hand-built in engine or exported as heavy sequences. Rive makes the event lobby above breathe (water shimmer, lantern sway, pet idle loops) at a fraction of the size and iteration cost, and designers edit it without an engineer.

        
Who uses it
UI artists and motion designers author; client engineers integrate the runtime once.

        
Limitations
Vector-first, so painterly 3D renders still enter as raster layers; team needs a short state-machine learning curve.

        
IMPACT
High
Live-feeling event UI on every screen, days instead of sprints per animated surface.

      

      

        
Meshy

        
Image-to-3D for a 3D game

        
Tested for this submission
I fed the approved 2D Mango into Meshy 6 and got back a 3D base mesh in minutes, same silhouette, lei and pose. An actual model you can spin and light, not a mockup. It is the concept-to-3D gap starting to close.

        

          
          →
          
        

        
Minutes from 2D keeper to a recognizable 3D base mesh. It is a blockout the character team still retopologizes and rigs, not a shippable asset. What it saves is the head start.

        
What it does
Turns concept images into textured 3D meshes with PBR maps and auto-retopology options. The approved 2D pet renders in Part 2 become posable 3D drafts in minutes.

        
Why this one
This is a 3D Bingo game. The expensive part is not the concept art, it is turning that concept into a model. Image-to-3D collapses the roughest early stretch of that work, so 3D artists start from a moving draft instead of a blank viewport, and marketing gets turnaround renders from any angle immediately.

        
Who uses it
3D character and prop artists for blockouts; UA team for quick-angle renders of approved characters.

        
Limitations
Output is a draft, never ship-ready: topology and rigging still need professional passes. It is best used as the sketching stage of 3D work.

        
IMPACT
Medium
Cuts character blockout time from days to hours and multiplies how many concepts reach 3D review.

      

    

  







  

    
BonusBeyond the Brief

    

    
Prompt DNA: art direction
as versioned tokens.

    
The idea behind everything above, written up as a studio system. Plus a second artifact already built and in use.

    

      

        
The system

        
Every event gets a DNA file: frozen style tokens with version numbers, owned by the art director, stored in git like code. Prompts are assembled from tokens, never written freehand. When the AD updates a token, every downstream prompt template inherits it, and the version stamp travels into each asset's metadata, so any rendered image can be traced back to the exact style state that produced it.

        

# summer-festival.dna.yml · v1.3 · owner: art director

STYLE:  glossy stylized 3D render, Pixar-like casual mobile game asset

LIGHT:  warm golden-hour lighting, subtle rim light

FORM:   big expressive friendly eyes, soft rounded shapes

TECH:   subsurface scattering, glossy highlights, high detail

PALETTE: FFC24B FF9E45 FF6F61 FF5C7A 1FB8A6 F3D9A4 3A2A5C FFF4E2

# character prompt = STYLE + [CHARACTER] + FORM + TECH + LIGHT

# changing a token bumps the version; assets log the version they used
        

        
This is why 45 character generations across 5 pets read as one family, and why the next event starts from a system instead of a blank prompt.

      

      

        
Already shipped alongside this test

        
Iteration Log, a working tool built for this submission: drag-and-drop gallery where every generation gets a verdict (kept, rejected, needs fix), its prompt, and its reason. Filterable, sortable, exportable.

        
It exists because the assignment asks for rejected outcomes, and most creatives lose exactly that history. Logging during the work, instead of reconstructing it afterwards, is a habit worth building in. The rejects sharpen the next prompt, and the reasons help onboard the next hire.

        Open the Iteration Log↗
        
55 entries seeded from Part 2 · filter, reorder, edit, export JSON · runs in the browser, no backend

        
Together the two form a loop: DNA defines what to generate, the log records what happened, and the deltas feed the next DNA version. That loop is the creative evaluation framework this bonus is really about.
